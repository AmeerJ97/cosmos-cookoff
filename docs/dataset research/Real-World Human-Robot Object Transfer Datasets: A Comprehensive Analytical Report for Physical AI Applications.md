# **Real-World Human-Robot Object Transfer Datasets: A Comprehensive Analytical Report for Physical AI Applications**

## **Introduction to the Physics and Dynamics of Object Transfer**

The seamless physical transfer of objects between human operators and robotic agents represents one of the most complex, highly coupled dynamical challenges in the field of embodied artificial intelligence. Unlike rigid pick-and-place operations—where a robot interacts with static objects in deterministic, highly structured environments—a handover is a continuous, stochastic physical and social interaction. It requires the instantaneous synchronization of spatial kinematics, the real-time modulation of non-linear grip forces, the interpretation of implicit behavioral cues such as human gaze and arm deceleration, and the continuous estimation of load-sharing between the giver and the receiver. For autonomous systems to operate safely and effectively in human-centric environments—spanning industrial assembly floors, healthcare facilities, and domestic spaces—they must achieve a level of physical fluency that mirrors human intuition.

Historically, the development of robotic handover capabilities relied almost exclusively on heuristic, rule-based control systems or synthetic simulation environments. Controllers were mathematically constrained by rigid impedance and admittance models that dictated exactly how a robot should react to force vectors. While simulations and synthetic generation engines provide massive data scalability, they frequently fail to capture the subtle stochasticity of human behavior, the non-linear friction dynamics of diverse object surfaces (such as skin deformation against plastic or metal), and the inherent noise profiles of physical sensors operating in the real world. This phenomenon, known as the sim-to-real gap, remains a persistent bottleneck in contact-rich manipulation tasks.

Consequently, the robotics and machine learning communities have increasingly pivoted toward large-scale, purely real-world datasets. These datasets provide the empirical, noisy, and high-fidelity grounding necessary to train robust, data-driven, and generative vision-language-action (VLA) models capable of generalizing across novel objects and unstructured environments. By capturing exact force-torque signatures, multi-view RGB-D video, and precise joint encoder telemetry, researchers can map the underlying physics of a handover directly into the latent space of a neural network.

This exhaustive report provides an in-depth analysis of real-world human-robot handoff, object transfer, and collaborative handover datasets. It is strictly curated to exclude synthetic, simulated, or generated data, focusing entirely on physical interactions captured through empirical sensor arrays, multi-view video, and precise motion tracking. The insights detailed herein are designed to serve as a foundational, encyclopedic resource for the development of advanced reasoning agents, particularly within the context of high-stakes, compute-heavy competitive frameworks such as the NVIDIA Cosmos Cookoff.

## **Foundational Human-Human Interaction Baselines for Kinematic Modeling**

Before a robotic system can effectively and safely hand an object to a human, the fundamental physics and mechanics of human-to-human handovers must be modeled and understood. Human handovers serve as the biological gold standard for this task, characterized by sub-second latency, optimal jerk minimization, fluid load transfer, and seamless non-verbal communication. Datasets capturing these human-to-human interactions provide the critical kinematic priors and force thresholds necessary for training human-inspired robotic controllers.

### **The Multimodal Data Set of Human Handovers (Khanna et al.)**

To inspire fluid human-robot interaction and provide a mathematical baseline for load transfer, researchers have meticulously documented the physical and kinematic properties of human-to-human handovers.8 The Multimodal Data Set of Human Handovers, developed by Khanna, Björkman, and Smith at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, represents one of the most rigorous empirical captures of this specific phenomenon.10 It provides 8,672 distinct handover events recorded at an exceptionally high frequency of 120 Hz, ensuring that the micro-dynamics of the release phase are captured with millisecond precision.8

The dataset's architecture is built around a highly controlled, laboratory-grade motion capture (MoCap) environment involving 13 participant pairs, yielding 26 total participants.8 The physical transfer medium is a specialized baton explicitly engineered to measure the complex forces of a handover.12 This baton is equipped with three highly sensitive Onrobot HEX 6-axis force-torque sensors.12 Two sensors are positioned on the extremities to record the independent grip forces of the giver and the receiver, while a central sensor records the interaction forces and torques (Fx, Fy, Fz, Tx, Ty, Tz) during the overlapping phase of the transfer.12 The grip forces can be mathematically derived from the \-Fz component of the giver and taker wrench profiles, providing a continuous time-series representation of load sharing.12

The spatial kinematics of the interaction are captured using an OptiTrack MoCap system running MOTIVE 3.0 software, utilizing an advanced array of 12 cameras, comprising eight PrimeX13 cameras placed along the room's perimeter and four PrimeX13W Wide-angle cameras positioned in the corners.12 This setup yields highly precise spatial positional data (x, y, z) and rotational orientation encoded in quaternions (q0, q1, q2, q3) for the baton.12 Furthermore, it captures a 13-frame skeletal representation of the upper body movements for both the giver and the taker, tracking the exact postural adjustments humans make as they reach across interpersonal space.12

The experimental design introduces critical variables that test the boundaries of handover mechanics. It includes controlled trials with standard batons weighing 0.8 kg and heavy batons weighing 1.8 kg, generating empirical data on how humans intuitively modulate their approach velocity and grip force relative to payload weight.12 Most crucially, the dataset incorporates "visual impairment" trials, where the giver's vision is intentionally restricted.12 This forces the giver to rely entirely on haptic and force-torque feedback to determine the exact moment the receiver has taken the load.

The inclusion of visually impaired trials provides a critical analog for robotic systems operating under sensor occlusion. In real-world industrial or domestic environments, a robot's line of sight to an object is frequently blocked by the human receiver's arm, body, or the object itself. By training machine learning models on how visually impaired humans utilize specific force-torque thresholds to trigger a grip release, developers can construct robotic control policies that seamlessly transition from vision-dominant spatial planning to haptic-dominant reasoning during the final, critical milliseconds of a physical handover. This multi-sensory fallback mechanism is essential for the robust physical AI reasoning required by platforms like Cosmos Reason 2\.

### **The HOH (Human-Object-Human) Markerless Dataset**

While the Khanna dataset excels in providing high-precision, high-frequency force tracking using a standardized and instrumented baton, real-world robotic deployment requires generalization across highly diverse, uninstrumented object geometries. The HOH (Human-Object-Human) Markerless Dataset, developed by researchers at Clarkson University, addresses this limitation by focusing extensively on category-level object diversity and markerless motion capture.10

Scaling up precise human-object interaction data is notoriously challenging, as traditional marker-based MoCap systems are expensive, intrusive, and difficult to scale across thousands of distinct objects involved in household rearrangement.16 The HOH dataset bypasses these limitations through an expansive multi-camera setup. The dataset captures 2,720 distinct handover events across a massive repository of 136 unique household objects.15 It involves 40 participants organized into 20 pairs, with explicitly documented role reversals to capture behavioral variances between acting as a giver versus a receiver.15

The data capture architecture relies on a fully markerless, 360-degree environmental setup. This is achieved through a multi-view sensor array consisting of four Kinect RGB-D cameras operating at 30 frames per second, complemented by four FLIR RGB cameras operating at 60 frames per second.15 The resulting data density is immense, yielding 2.8 million high-resolution RGB images, 1.6 million OpenPose skeletal tracking maps extracted from the Kinect videos, and 250,000 fused spatiotemporal 3D point clouds.15

The ground truth annotations provided in the HOH dataset are exhaustive and highly structured. They detail specific temporal events, including exact grasp, transfer, and release timestamps.15 Furthermore, the dataset provides ground truth annotations for multi-person handedness and categorizes every single interaction into a highly specific 28-class grasp taxonomy.15 To facilitate computer vision training, the dataset includes 2.5 million tracked segments for the giver's hand, the receiver's hand, and the object itself within the Kinect RGB streams, along with 790,000 specific 3D point cloud segments and complete 3D geometric models and metadata for all 136 objects.15

The unprecedented scale of 136 distinct objects forces machine learning models to infer grasp affordances based on dynamic object geometry and semantic categorization, rather than relying on memorized physical templates. The spatiotemporal 3D point clouds provided in HOH are particularly valuable for training the spatial intelligence modules within a VLA architecture. By correlating the 28-class grasp types with the 3D geometric point cloud of the object, an AI agent can predict exactly how a human is likely to hold a specific object—for instance, understanding that a human will instinctively hold a mug by its handle rather than its rim. The robotic agent can subsequently plan a complementary, non-colliding grasp vector that leaves sufficient surface area for the human receiver, ensuring an ergonomically sound and natural transfer.

## **Direct Human-Robot Interaction and Anomaly Detection**

While human-human kinematic data provides the ideal mathematical template for fluid motion, the physical embodiment of a robot introduces inherent asymmetries in speed, reach, rigidness, and sociability. Datasets capturing direct, empirical human-robot interaction are therefore indispensable for identifying and mitigating the physical and psychological friction inherent in human-machine collaboration.

### **The 3HANDS Dataset: Supernumerary Robotic Limbs**

The 3HANDS dataset introduces a novel and highly complex paradigm in the realm of object transfer: physical interactions involving Supernumerary Robotic Limbs (SRLs).18 SRLs are wearable robotic structures integrated closely with the user's body, designed to augment human physical capabilities and acting as a literal "third arm".20 This physical closeness necessitates a level of seamless, naturalistic human-machine interaction that far exceeds the requirements of traditional isolated robotics.20

Unlike stationary collaborative robots (cobots) mounted on industrial workbenches, SRLs operate continuously within the intimate personal space of the human user.18 The 3HANDS dataset captures handovers between a primary participant performing a continuous daily activity and another participant enacting the motions of a hip-mounted SRL in a naturalistic manner.20 The dataset encompasses rich interactions with 51 everyday objects of varying shapes, weights, and sizes.23

The data specifically captures the unique interaction characteristics inherent to body-mounted robotics, including highly asymmetric object origins, implicit motion synchronization without direct visual contact, and the heavy cognitive load of the user's engagement in a primary task during the moment of handover.20 Utilizing advanced motion capture markers, the dataset provides full 3D body shapes and poses, articulated face and hand meshes, and precise 3D object poses.23 This allows for the exact computational modeling of physical contact between the human body and the object over time.23

The 3HANDS dataset is instrumental for exploring the poorly understood domain of proxemics and personal space in human-robot interaction.18 Traditional robotics datasets assume a clear spatial separation and safety boundary between the human and the machine. 3HANDS forces the machine learning model to reason about complex collision avoidance, joint limits, and naturalistic trajectory generation in highly constrained, intimate, and constantly shifting spaces. For developers utilizing open-world foundation models, the 3HANDS dataset offers the rare opportunity to train agents that can anticipate human intent while the human is simultaneously multi-tasking. This trains the network to execute handovers that require zero cognitive overhead or gaze-shifting from the human user, maximizing the efficiency of the collaborative workflow.

### **Multimodal Handover Failure Detection Dataset (Thoduka et al.)**

A critical and pervasive oversight in many robotic learning pipelines, particularly those driven by imitation learning or behavioral cloning, is the exclusive reliance on successful, highly curated demonstration data. Safe physical AI requires models that can not only execute a task but also anticipate, detect, and autonomously recover from physical failures in real-time. The Multimodal Handover Failure Detection Dataset, introduced by Thoduka, Hochgeschwender, Gall, and Plöger at the 2024 IEEE International Conference on Robotics and Automation (ICRA), addresses this critical gap by explicitly capturing anomalous, dangerous, and failed interactions.25

This dataset contains meticulously annotated video feeds, continuous force-torque readings, and high-frequency robot joint states for both successful and explicitly failed robot-to-human (R2H) and human-to-robot (H2R) handovers.25 Crucially, the failures documented within the dataset are predominantly caused by the human participant, simulating the extreme unpredictability of real-world deployment.25 These failures include scenarios such as premature grip release, sudden multi-directional pulling, complete target misses, and hesitations.

Baseline evaluations conducted on the dataset utilized neural networks, including a video classification method using 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and a temporal action segmentation approach.26 The results empirically demonstrated that while annotated video is essential for macro-level action classification, the integration of force-torque sensor data and precise gripper positional data drastically improves failure detection latency and action segmentation accuracy, achieving a maximum early-detection accuracy of 71%.26

The inclusion of these specific failure modes is a paradigm-shifting asset for developing robust AI guardrails. In a standard Vision-Language Model training regimen, the network learns to map a semantic instruction to a successful kinematic trajectory. However, the Thoduka dataset provides the mathematical "negative space" required for contrastive learning and anomaly detection.29 By training a model to recognize the early precursor signals of a failed handover—such as a sudden, erratic spike in the Tz torque axis indicating the human is twisting the object before establishing a firm friction grip—the robotic control system can proactively abort the release sequence. This microsecond-level decision-making prevents the object from dropping, mitigating damage to the payload and potential injury to the human. This dataset is foundational for implementing the rigorous safety and failure-recovery mechanisms expected in enterprise-grade physical AI systems deployed in the NVIDIA Cosmos framework.

### **HAGS: Hand and Glove Segmentation Dataset**

In industrial manufacturing and heavy robotic collaboration, human hands are rarely bare. They are frequently obscured by heavy protective equipment, altering the visual profile of the hand and dampening haptic feedback. Traditional datasets lack the specific visual domain representations required for these scenarios. The HAGS (Hand and Glove Segmentation) Dataset provides a targeted solution by supplying challenging examples specifically engineered to build applications toward hand and glove segmentation in industrial human-robot collaboration scenarios.30

The dataset contains 191 high-definition videos of complex joint assembly tasks, totaling over 9 hours of continuous content.30 Crucially, it provides 1,728 frames of exacting, pixel-level labels.30 The dataset explicitly features multiclass segmentation designed to distinguish between left and right hands, both gloved and ungloved, across ten diverse participants.30 Furthermore, it includes out-of-distribution images constructed via green screen augmentations to rigorously determine the robustness of Machine Learning classifiers.30 For a reasoning VLA model, the ability to accurately segment a gloved hand from the surrounding industrial machinery is a prerequisite for generating a safe approach vector during an object transfer on a manufacturing line.

## **Scale and Embodiment: Advancing to Bimanual and Humanoid Platforms**

The transition from single-arm, fixed-base industrial manipulators to mobile, bimanual, and fully humanoid robots represents the next major evolutionary leap in embodied AI. Handover tasks in this domain are exponentially more complex, requiring dynamic whole-body coordination, continuous balancing, and complex bimanual load distribution. Recent large-scale datasets provide the sheer volume and diversity required to train generalized foundation models for these advanced robotic embodiments.

### **RealSource World Dataset (RealMan Robotics)**

Released to directly address the global industry shortage of fully aligned, real-world robotic data, the RealSource World dataset is a massive, open-source multi-modal repository focused intensely on dual-arm humanoid robotics and bimanual manipulation.31 Collected using the RS-02 dual-arm humanoid robot, this dataset breaks entrenched data silos by providing an unprecedented scale of real-world, physical interaction.33

The scale and completeness of RealSource World are staggering. The dataset encompasses 35 distinct, long-horizon manipulation tasks resulting in 11,428 continuous episodes, generating over 14 million frames of real-world dual-arm manipulation demonstration data.33 To ensure broad generalizability, the dataset was meticulously captured within 10 physical, real-world scenario zones located in a dedicated Humanoid Robot Data Training Center, spanning environments such as smart homes, eldercare facilities, agriculture, new retail, automotive assembly, and catering.35

The raw data modalities boast a 100% modality completeness metric and a 78% noise-resistance quality rating.31 Observations include synchronized multi-camera visual feeds (head, left hand, and right hand RGB views), a massive 57-dimensional proprioceptive state space encompassing all joint angles, continuous force data, end-effector poses, and the corresponding action commands.31 Furthermore, the dataset includes fine-grained human annotations with atomic skill segmentation, complete camera parameters (both intrinsics and extrinsics) for all episodes, and an overarching quality assessment for every single trajectory.33 All data and code are released under a permissive CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license.33

The 57-dimensional proprioceptive state space, combined with the tri-view camera setups, allows for the training of highly sophisticated latent action representations. When a dual-arm humanoid robot hands a large object—such as a loaded tray or a heavy automotive component—to a human, it must continuously shift its center of gravity to prevent falling as the payload weight leaves its end-effectors. The atomic skill segmentation provided in RealSource allows models to isolate the precise mathematical sub-task of "object transfer" from the broader context of "fetching and delivering." This enables researchers to fine-tune Cosmos foundation models specifically on the high-dimensional kinematics of the release phase, ensuring dynamic stability.

### **AgiBot World Colosseo Dataset**

Operating at an even grander scale of deployment, the AgiBot World Colosseo platform is a scalable and highly intelligent embodied system dataset focusing on sophisticated bimanual manipulation and multi-robot synergy.36 It was curated using an entire fleet of 100 dual-arm humanoid robots (specifically the AgiBot G1).37

The dataset contains over 1 million trajectories captured across 4,000 square meters of highly detailed, 1:1 scale physical environments.37 These environments accurately reconstruct five major real-world domains: domestic, retail, industrial, restaurant, and office spaces.37 It features complex interactions with over 3,000 unique objects, ensuring extreme visual and geometric diversity.37

Data collection for these tasks utilizes an all-purpose, highly advanced sensor setup. The mobile base humanoid robots are equipped with whole-body control capabilities, integrated RGB-D cameras, visuo-tactile sensors, and advanced 6-DoF dexterous hands.37 AgiBot World explicitly focuses on long-horizon tasks—often spanning 30 to 60 seconds of continuous operation—requiring multiple atomic skills and complex multi-robot synergistic collaboration.37 The data pipeline ensures exceptional quality through a rigorous human-in-the-loop verification protocol, filtering out suboptimal trajectories before they enter the training distribution.36

The sheer scale of AgiBot World makes it an ideal, foundational candidate for pre-training large-scale robot policies. The inclusion of 6-DoF dexterous hands and visuo-tactile sensors elevates the physical fidelity of the handover data far beyond simple parallel-jaw grippers. Dexterous hands allow for complex in-hand manipulation prior to the handover, enabling the robot to re-orient the object—for example, rotating the handle of a hammer to point directly toward the human's open palm—before the transfer occurs. Training a reasoning agent on this dataset imbues the system with advanced spatial and semantic awareness, ensuring that handovers are not just physically stable, but ergonomically optimized for the human receiver.

### **BridgeData V2 and Open X-Embodiment**

While primarily recognized as massive generalist datasets for robotic manipulation, both BridgeData V2 and the broader Open X-Embodiment dataset contain specific, highly relevant subsets dedicated to human-robot handovers.39

The Open X-Embodiment Dataset represents the largest open-source real robot dataset to date, aggregating over 1 million real robot trajectories spanning 22 diverse robot embodiments.39 Pooled from 60 existing robot datasets across 34 global robotic research labs, it demonstrates over 500 skills and 150,000 specific tasks.39 Models trained on this massive, cross-embodiment data—such as RT-2-X—exhibit highly effective positive transfer, improving the capabilities of single robots by leveraging the diverse kinematic experiences of other platforms.39

BridgeData V2 specifically contributes 60,096 trajectories across 24 distinct environments, collected primarily via human teleoperation on a WidowX 250 6DOF robot arm.37 Within its extensive, natural-language-labeled task taxonomy, BridgeData V2 explicitly evaluates complex interaction tasks such as "Fruit Handover" and "Bottle Handoff".43 The data is captured via an over-the-shoulder RGB-D camera, two randomized RGB cameras, and a crucial wrist-mounted wide-angle RGB camera.41

BridgeData V2 serves as a vital statistical benchmark for evaluating the generalizability of manipulation policies across distinct semantic states. Because the dataset includes both isolated object manipulation (e.g., "Single Fruit Pick" with 400 steps) and collaborative transfer (e.g., "Fruit Handover" with 500 steps), it provides a unique, highly controlled variable.43 Researchers can directly analyze how a VLA model mathematically alters its approach vector, its velocity profile, and its terminal grip policies when a human agent is dynamically introduced into the scene, isolating the specific algorithmic adjustments required to transition from solitary manipulation to safe human-robot collaboration.

## **Egocentric Perspectives and Vision-Language-Action (VLA) Integration**

As artificial intelligence models evolve to tightly integrate natural language processing with complex visual reasoning and low-level motor control, training datasets must provide exceedingly rich textual annotations seamlessly aligned with physical actions. Egocentric (first-person) datasets are particularly valuable in this domain, as they closely mimic the exact sensory perspective of a mobile robot or an autonomous agent actively navigating a human environment.

### **HoloAssist: Egocentric Human Interaction and Intervention**

The HoloAssist dataset is a monumental, highly complex collection designed specifically for training interactive AI assistants in the physical world.45 It contains a massive 166 hours of recordings featuring 222 diverse participants.47 These participants form 350 unique instructor-performer pairs collaboratively executing 20 highly demanding, object-centric physical manipulation tasks.47 These tasks span consumer electronics (e.g., manipulating a DSLR or Nespresso machine), large office equipment, intricate furniture assembly, and industrial equipment like circuit breakers and NavVis laser scanners.47

Data was captured using a sophisticated mixed-reality (AR) headset worn by the task performer, simultaneously recording seven distinct, highly synchronized sensor modalities: raw RGB video, depth maps, head pose, continuous 3D hand pose, eye gaze tracking, spatial audio, and Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) data.47 Crucially, an instructor observed the performer's first-person feed in real-time from a remote terminal, providing continuous verbal guidance and interventions.46

The dataset is augmented with comprehensive third-person manual annotations that tag specific action segments, explicitly identify physical mistakes, and categorize the nature of the instructor's verbal interventions.47 HoloAssist effectively bridges the semantic gap between verbal instruction and physical execution. The inclusion of eye gaze and head pose data offers profound insights into human attention mechanisms during collaborative tasks. In a physical handover scenario, humans implicitly signal their readiness by rapidly shifting their gaze from the object to the receiver's hand, or vice versa. By mathematically correlating eye gaze vectors with 3D hand poses and verbal instructions, developers can train AI agents to interpret human attention dynamically. A robot powered by a Cosmos reasoning model could utilize this exact data to dynamically pause or delay a handover if the human's gaze indicates distraction, thereby drastically reducing the probability of an accidental drop.

### **InterVLA: Human-Object-Human Vision-Language-Action Schema**

InterVLA is a large-scale human-object-human interaction dataset structured specifically from the ground up to support the Vision-Language-Action (VLA) neural network paradigm.48 It comprises 3,906 highly refined interaction sequences, totaling 11.4 hours and 1.2 million frames of rich multimodal data.48

The dataset captures complex interactions where an assistant provides direct physical services to an instructor based strictly on egocentric perception and verbal commands.48 It utilizes a unique four-tuple data structure combining egocentric RGB videos, exocentric (third-person) RGB videos, language instructions, and high-precision human/object motions.48

The dual-perspective (egocentric and exocentric) synchronized video, combined with nuanced verbal commands, is tailor-made for training next-generation VLA models. In collaborative tasks, human language is often highly contextual and deictic (e.g., "Hand me *that* wrench, no, the *larger* one"). InterVLA forces the neural network to ground ambiguous language commands in a dynamic, shifting 3D physical space, resolving intent by mapping the verbal instruction directly to the exocentric spatial relationship between the two humans and the target object. This dataset is absolutely critical for moving beyond rigid, heavily scripted robotic handovers to fluid, dialogue-driven, and context-aware object transfers.

### **RoboVQA and Vlaser: Multimodal Long-Horizon Reasoning**

To support advanced, temporally aware reasoning agents, the RoboVQA dataset (and its expansive integration into the broader Vlaser dataset architecture) provides a massive, unprecedented repository of question-answer pairs grounded specifically in robotic actions and physics.50 The Vlaser dataset integrates 1.5 million high-quality QA pairs explicitly for embodied grounding tasks—such as predicting highly accurate bounding boxes from open-vocabulary descriptions and localizing object center points—and an additional 1.2 million QA pairs dedicated entirely to general robotic Visual Question Answering (RoboVQA).50

The dataset places a heavy emphasis on long-horizon reasoning and spatial intelligence, featuring precise, step-by-step planning annotations spanning 102 diverse scenes, 12 distinct robot embodiments, and 107 atomic tasks.50 Handover tasks in real-world deployments are rarely isolated, discrete actions; they are almost universally atomic steps embedded within a much longer-horizon plan (e.g., assembling an engine block or cooking a complex meal). The RoboVQA dataset enables transformer-based models to maintain deep temporal context. A robot must understand not just *how* to physically hand over a screwdriver, but *why* the human needs it at this specific, critical moment in the assembly sequence. By training extensively on RoboVQA, a Cosmos-powered agent can infer the overarching semantic task context, allowing it to proactively fetch and orient tools optimally for the human's immediate next step, thereby vastly accelerating the collaborative workflow and reducing human fatigue.

## **Cross-Dataset Meta-Analysis: Algorithmic Design for Physical AI**

The aggregation and synthesis of these real-world datasets reveal critical macro-trends in the mathematical and algorithmic development of physical AI. These insights directly dictate how neural network architectures must be structured to achieve robust, safe human-robot collaboration.

### **The Algorithmic Inadequacy of Purely Visual Modalities**

A recurring, undeniable theme across the failure detection research 28 and the high-fidelity human-baseline datasets 12 is the fundamental insufficiency of RGB video alone for executing safe, closed-loop handovers. While high-resolution visual data is exceptionally useful for macro-level trajectory planning, obstacle avoidance, and semantic object recognition, it fails entirely during the terminal phase of an object transfer. During the critical "handoff window"—the milliseconds during which the physical load is transferred from one agent's end-effector to another—visual occlusion is practically guaranteed by the converging geometries of the human hand and the robotic gripper.

The integration of force-torque (F/T) data, such as the continuous 6D wrench data provided in the Khanna dataset 12 and the high-fidelity visuo-tactile sensory inputs prevalent in the AgiBot World architecture 37, is strictly non-negotiable for safe deployment. Machine learning models must be trained to process high-frequency haptic streams (frequently operating at 120 Hz or higher) to detect minute micro-slips and continuously monitor the Z-axis force profile against gravity. The artificial intelligence must mathematically reason that a drop in the robot's perceived load (Fz) corresponding with a highly stable spatial coordinate implies the human has successfully taken the weight, safely triggering the command to open the grippers. Physical AI models built within the Cosmos framework must utilize these multi-modal datasets to develop dynamic sensory fallback policies, seamlessly shifting attention weights within the transformer architecture from visual tokens to haptic tokens as the spatial distance between the robot and human approaches zero.

### **Implicit Social Signaling and Kinematic Synchronization**

Human handovers are deeply communicative, functioning as a synchronized biological dance. The datasets capturing precise upper-body kinematics (Khanna et al.) 12, continuous eye gaze tracking (HoloAssist) 47, and personal space intrusions (3HANDS) 20 unequivocally highlight that handovers are governed by implicit social contracts. Humans signal their definitive intent to release an object through minute, predictable decelerations of the arm, specific changes in muscular tension, and highly directed eye contact.

Robotic control systems trained solely on rigid, teleoperated pick-and-place trajectories—where the human is treated mathematically as a static, unmoving receptacle—result in jarring, unnatural, and highly mechanical interactions that cause hesitation, discomfort, and significant cognitive strain for the human user. By leveraging the advanced interaction sequences in the InterVLA 48 and 3HANDS datasets, reasoning agents can actively learn the complex kinematics of naturalistic synchronization. A Cosmos-powered model can learn to generate algorithmic approach trajectories that perfectly mimic human velocity profiles (such as generating a smooth, bell-shaped velocity curve that minimizes jerk) and utilize its end-effector orientation to clearly communicate its intended handover location, rapidly establishing trust and physical fluency.

### **Bypassing the Sim-to-Real Gap with Real-to-Real Transfer**

A foundational constraint of this specific analysis is the total exclusion of synthetic and simulated data. While modern simulation engines have achieved remarkable visual and rigid-body physical accuracy, they inherently suffer from the well-documented "sim-to-real gap." Simulators fundamentally struggle to accurately model the complex, non-linear friction of a human hand, the unpredictable physical deformation of a soft object (like a cloth, a plastic bottle, or a piece of fruit), and the irrational, highly spontaneous movements of a distracted human worker in a cluttered environment.

The massive, unprecedented scale of real-world datasets like RealSource World (14 million frames) 33 and AgiBot World (1 million trajectories) 38 presents a massive paradigm shift: Real-to-Real learning. By exposing high-capacity foundation models directly to a vast, uncurated distribution of empirical, noisy, real-world data, the model learns generalized, highly robust feature representations without inheriting the mathematical biases and limitations of a physics engine. The extreme diversity of 3,000 objects in AgiBot 37 or 136 objects in HOH 15 ensures that the model learns the underlying, universal physics of grasping, friction, and load transfer, rather than merely memorizing the specific geometry of a simulated CAD model.

## **Strategic Deployment Pathways for the NVIDIA Cosmos Cookoff**

For developers and researchers participating in the NVIDIA Cosmos Cookoff, the ultimate objective is to architect projects that tackle complex, real-world physical AI reasoning problems utilizing the Cosmos Reason 2 model.2 The real-world datasets extensively detailed in this report provide the optimal raw, empirical material for several highly competitive, leaderboard-topping project architectures:

1. **Safety-Critical Handoff Agents:** By ingeniously combining the failure anomaly data from Thoduka et al. 28 with the high-fidelity force-torque profiles from Khanna et al. 12, a highly competitive team could fine-tune a Cosmos model to act as a proactive, ultra-low-latency safety monitor. The agent would parse real-time multimodal streams to predict and actively prevent drops in industrial human-robot collaboration zones, serving as a critical safety override layer that reacts faster than human reflexes.  
2. **Language-Conditioned Dexterous Transfer:** Leveraging the rich four-tuple alignments in the InterVLA 48 and the massive spatial reasoning pairs in RoboVQA 50 datasets, developers can build a highly sophisticated reasoning brain capable of parsing complex, highly ambiguous verbal instructions (e.g., "Pass me the heavy drill carefully, handle first, watch out for the wire"). The model would translate these nuanced semantics into dynamic, ergonomically safe robotic handover trajectories that adapt to the human's real-time position.  
3. **Humanoid Synergistic Collaboration:** Utilizing the vast, continuous dual-arm trajectories from RealSource World 33 or the visuo-tactile bimanual data from AgiBot World 38, a team could push the absolute boundaries of bimanual humanoid manipulation. Cosmos Reason 2 could be extensively post-trained to manage the incredibly complex mathematical load distribution required when a humanoid robot and a human collaboratively carry and transfer a bulky, awkwardly shaped object, constantly and dynamically adjusting to the human's unpredictable gait and balance shifts.

The successful execution of a human-robot object transfer remains a definitive, highly visible benchmark for the overall efficacy of physical AI. It demands the flawless synthesis of high-level semantic reasoning, real-time spatial awareness, and low-level, high-frequency motor control. The permanent transition away from heuristic simulations toward massive, real-world, multimodal datasets provides the empirical, noisy foundation necessary to solve these challenges. By grounding open-world foundation models in the complex, highly nuanced reality of empirical physical interaction, developers can engineer autonomous systems capable of true physical reasoning, seamlessly integrating robots into the collaborative fabric of human society.
